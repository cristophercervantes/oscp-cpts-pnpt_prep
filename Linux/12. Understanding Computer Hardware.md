### 1. The Motherboard & CPU

The **motherboard** (system board) is the primary circuit board. Everything else connects to it.

- **The CPU (Processor):** The "brain" of the computer.
    
- **Architecture Types:**
    
    - **x86 (32-bit):** Older. Limited to **4 GB of RAM**. Examples: i386, i486, i686.
        
    - **x86_64 (64-bit):** Modern standard. Can use massive amounts of RAM and is more secure. Backward compatible with 32-bit.
        
- **Multi-core vs. Multiprocessor:**
    
    - **Multi-core:** One physical chip with multiple "brains" inside.
        
    - **Multiprocessor:** Multiple physical chips on one motherboard.
        

> **Commands to Know:**
> 
> - `arch`: Shows the architecture (e.g., `x86_64`).
>     
> - `lscpu`: Shows detailed CPU info (cores, threads, vendor ID).
>     

### 2. Memory: RAM vs. Swap

Programs must be loaded into memory to run.

- **RAM (Physical):** Fast, volatile storage.
    
- **Swap (Virtual):** A designated area on the **Hard Drive**.
    
    - **How it works:** When RAM is full, Linux moves inactive data to the Swap space. This prevents the system from crashing but slows performance.
        

> **Commands to Know:**
> 
> - `free`: Shows total, used, and free RAM/Swap.
>     
> - `free -m`: Shows output in **Megabytes**.
>     
> - `free -g`: Shows output in **Gigabytes**.
>     

### 3. Connection Buses

Buses are the "highways" that allow data to travel between the CPU and peripherals.

- **PCI (Peripheral Component Interconnect):** Used for internal cards (Video, Network, Storage controllers). Usually **Cold-plug** (must turn off power to change).
    
- **USB (Universal Serial Bus):** Used for external devices. **Hot-plug** (connect/disconnect while running).
    

> **Commands to Know:**
> 
> - `lspci`: Lists all internal PCI devices (Video cards, NICs).
>     
> - `lsusb`: Lists all connected USB devices (Mouse, Hubs, Storage).


##  4. Storage Devices (Hard Drives & SSDs)

In Linux, all storage devices are represented as **device files** in the `/dev` directory.

### Partitioning Standards

Before a drive can be used, it must be partitioned.

- **MBR (Master Boot Record):** The legacy standard. Supports up to 4 primary partitions and drives up to 2TB.
    
- **GPT (GUID Partition Table):** The modern standard. Supports more partitions and drives larger than 2TB.
    

### Device Naming Convention

Linux names drives based on their connection type and the order they were detected:

1. **Prefix:** `sd` (SATA, SCSI, USB) or `hd` (Older IDE).
    
2. **Letter:** The disk order (a, b, c...).
    
3. **Number:** The partition on that disk (1, 2, 5...).
    

> **Example:** `/dev/sdb1` is the **first partition** on the **second SATA/USB drive**.

### Tools to Manage Disks

- **MBR Tools:** `fdisk`, `cfdisk`, `sfdisk`.
    
- **GPT Tools:** `gdisk`, `cgdisk`, `sgdisk`.
    
- **Universal Tools:** `parted` (Command line) and `gparted` (Graphical).
    

## 5. Solid State Disks (SSD) vs. HDD

- **HDD (Hard Disk Drive):** Uses spinning platters and moving heads. Slower and more prone to physical damage.
    
- **SSD (Solid State Disk):** No moving parts (uses flash memory).
    
    - **Pros:** Faster boot times, less heat, silent, lower power usage.
        
    - **Cons:** Higher cost per GB, limited upgradeability if soldered to the motherboard.
        

## 6. Optical & Removable Media

Devices like CD-ROMs, DVDs, and Blu-Rays are removable storage.

- **Mounting:** Linux attaches these to the filesystem, usually under `/media/` or `/mnt/`.
    
- **Safety:** Always **unmount** (`umount`) a drive before physically removing it to prevent data corruption.
    

## 7. Peripheral Management & Drivers

Hardware needs **drivers** (software) to talk to the OS.

- **Linux Drivers:** Often built directly into the **Kernel** or loaded as **Modules**.
    
- **Support:** If a manufacturer doesn't provide a Linux driver, the open-source community often creates one.
    
- **Tip:** Check the **HCL (Hardware Compatibility List)** for your distribution before buying new gear.
    

## 8. Video Display & Power

### Video Connectors

|**Type**|**Description**|
|---|---|
|**VGA**|Older 15-pin analog cable.|
|**DVI**|29-pin digital/analog interface.|
|**HDMI**|High-Definition; carries video and audio; supports 4K.|
|**DisplayPort (DP)**|Newest digital standard; Mini-DP common on Apple.|

### Power Supplies

Converts AC (wall power) to DC (computer power: 3.3v, 5v, 12v).

- **Quality Matters:** A cheap power supply can fail and destroy the rest of your hardware.
    
- **Laptops:** Have an advantage during power fluctuations because they can switch to battery power instantly.
